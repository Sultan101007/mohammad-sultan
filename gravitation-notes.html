Introduction to Gravitation

Newton's Apple Story

"In 1666, Newton saw an apple fall and wondered, 'Why did it fall straight down?' He realized a force must pull objects toward Earth, the same force that keeps planets in orbit. He named this force gravity."

Gravitation Definition:

"Gravitation is the force of attraction between any two bodies in the universe."

Newton's Law of Gravitation

Universal Law

"Every object in the universe attracts every other object with a force directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them."

\[F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}\]

Q: The Earth is attracted by Sun's gravity but doesn't fall into it. Why?

Answer: Earth's tangential velocity provides centripetal force that balances gravitational attraction, keeping it in orbit.

Q: If distance between two objects is halved, what happens to gravitational force?

Answer: Force becomes 4 times (since F ‚àù 1/r¬≤, half distance = 1/(1/2)¬≤ = 4 times).

Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion

Three Laws

1. Law of Orbits:

Planets move in elliptical orbits with Sun at one focus.

2. Law of Areas:

Line joining planet and Sun sweeps equal areas in equal times.

‚Üí Planet moves faster when closer to Sun

3. Law of Periods:

Square of orbital period ‚àù Cube of average distance from Sun

Thrust & Pressure

Definitions

Thrust: Force acting perpendicular to surface

Pressure: Thrust per unit area

\[\text{Pressure} = \frac{\text{Thrust}}{\text{Area}}\]

SI Unit: Pascal (Pa) = N/m¬≤

Applications

Q: Why sharp knife cuts better?

Answer: Smaller area ‚Üí Higher pressure with same force.

Q: Why camel has broad feet?

Answer: Larger area ‚Üí Lower pressure ‚Üí Doesn't sink in sand.

Q: Why nails have sharp ends?

Answer: Small area ‚Üí High pressure ‚Üí Easy to penetrate.

Buoyancy & Buoyant Force

Definition

"Buoyant force is the upward force exerted by fluid on immersed object. Also called upthrust."

Factors affecting buoyant force:
  • Density of fluid (‚Üë density ‚Üí ‚Üë buoyancy)
  • Volume of object submerged (‚Üë volume ‚Üí ‚Üë buoyancy)

Floating Conditions

Condition Result Density Relation
Buoyant force > Weight Object floats œÅ_object < œÅ_fluid
Buoyant force = Weight Object suspended œÅ_object = œÅ_fluid
Buoyant force < Weight Object sinks œÅ_object > œÅ_fluid
Q: Why easier to swim in sea than fresh water?

Answer: Sea water has higher density ‚Üí More buoyant force ‚Üí Easier to float.

Archimedes' Principle

Statement

"When an object is immersed in fluid, it experiences upward buoyant force equal to weight of fluid displaced by the object."

Buoyant force = Weight of fluid displaced

Applications

Submarine

Changes water in tanks to control buoyancy

Hot-air Balloon

Heated air less dense ‚Üí Rises

Ships

Displace large water volume to float

Real-life Applications

Astronomy

  • Planetary orbits: Gravity keeps planets in orbit
  • Tides: Moon's gravity causes sea tides
  • Satellites: Artificial satellites use orbital mechanics

Engineering

  • Building design: Must account for weight distribution
  • Bridge construction: Consider gravitational forces
  • Vehicle design: Center of gravity affects stability

Daily Life

  • Walking: Friction with ground prevents slipping
  • Falling objects: Always fall downward
  • Water flow: Gravity causes rivers to flow downhill
  • Weight measurement: Scales measure gravitational force
