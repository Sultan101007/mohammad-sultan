Introduction to Sound

What is Sound?

"Sound is a form of energy which creates a sensation of hearing when it reaches the ear."

  • Sound is produced by vibrating objects.
  • Vibration: Rapid back-and-forth motion of an object.
  • Law of conservation of energy applies to sound.
  • Requires a medium (solid, liquid, or gas) to travel.
Examples of Sound Production:

• Striking a tuning fork

• Vibrating vocal cords (human voice)

• Plucked guitar strings

Production of Sound

Medium Requirement

The substance through which sound travels is called a medium.

Solids

Fastest transmission

Example: Metal rods

Liquids

Medium speed

Example: Water

Gases

Slowest transmission

Example: Air

Important Note: It's the disturbance caused by vibrations that travels through the medium, NOT the particles themselves.

Bell-Jar Experiment Proof:

Electric bell in airtight jar with vacuum pump:

• Air present → Sound heard clearly

• Air removed → Sound becomes fainter

• Complete vacuum → No sound heard

Conclusion: Sound requires a medium to travel.

Types of Waves

Mechanical vs Electromagnetic

Mechanical Waves Electromagnetic Waves
Require a medium to propagate Do NOT require a medium
Example: Sound waves Example: Light waves
Cannot travel through vacuum Can travel through vacuum
Speed depends on medium Constant speed in vacuum (3×10⁸ m/s)

Sound waves are mechanical waves because they require a material medium (air, water, solids) to travel through.

Transverse vs Longitudinal Waves

Transverse Waves Longitudinal Waves
Particles oscillate perpendicular to wave direction Particles oscillate parallel to wave direction
Has crests and troughs Has compressions and rarefactions
Example: Light waves, water waves Example: Sound waves, slinky waves
Can travel without medium (light) Always requires medium
Demonstrated with rope Demonstrated with slinky

Sound as Longitudinal Waves

Compression & Rarefaction

Compression (C)

• Particles crowded together

• High density & pressure

• Represented by peaks

Rarefaction (R)

• Particles spread apart

• Low density & pressure

• Represented by troughs

Why sound waves are longitudinal: Particles of medium vibrate parallel to wave direction, creating alternating compressions and rarefactions that transfer sound energy.

Characteristics of Sound Waves

Basic Parameters

Characteristic Definition SI Unit Determines
Frequency (ν) Number of oscillations per second Hertz (Hz) Pitch (high/low)
Amplitude (A) Maximum displacement from mean position Meter (m) Loudness
Wavelength (λ) Distance between consecutive compressions or rarefactions Meter (m) Wave pattern
Time Period (T) Time for one complete oscillation Second (s) T = 1/ν
T = 1/ν    and    ν = 1/T

Sound Characteristics

Pitch

Brain's interpretation of frequency

High ν → High pitch (violin)

Low ν → Low pitch (drum)

Loudness

Ear's response to amplitude

Large A → Loud sound

Small A → Soft sound

Quality/Timbre

Distinguishes same pitch from different sources

Same note: Piano vs Guitar

Speed of Sound

Formula & Factors

v = ν × λ

Where: v = speed of sound, ν = frequency, λ = wavelength

Solids

Fastest

Aluminium: 6420 m/s

Steel: 5960 m/s

Liquids

Medium

Sea Water: 1531 m/s

Gases

Slowest

Air (0°C): 331 m/s

Air (22°C): 344 m/s

Factors affecting speed: Nature of medium (solid>liquid>gas), temperature (increases with temperature), density.

Speed Problems

Q: Sound wave frequency 2 kHz, wavelength 0.5 m. Calculate speed.

ν = 2 kHz = 2000 Hz, λ = 0.5 m

v = ν × λ = 2000 × 0.5 = 1000 m/s

Q: Thunder heard 4s after lightning. Speed of sound = 340 m/s. Distance to cloud?

Distance = Speed × Time

d = 340 × 4 = 1360 m

Q: Tuning fork frequency 500 Hz. Calculate time period.

T = 1/ν = 1/500 = 0.002 s = 2 ms

Reflection of Sound

Laws of Reflection

Like light, sound bounces back when it hits a hard surface.

Laws of Reflection (same as light):

1. Incident wave, reflected wave, and normal lie in same plane

2. Angle of reflection = Angle of incidence

Angle of Incidence (i)

Angle between incident ray and normal

Angle of Reflection (r)

Angle between reflected ray and normal

i = r

Echo & Reverberation

Echo

"Phenomenon where a sound produced is heard again due to reflection."

Conditions for distinct echo:

• Time interval ≥ 0.1s (sound persists in brain for 0.1s)

• Minimum distance to reflecting surface ≥ 17.2 m

• Multiple echoes possible due to multiple reflections

Q: Person hears echo after 2s. Speed of sound = 343 m/s. Distance to reflecting surface?

Distance = (Speed × Time)/2

d = (343 × 2)/2 = 343 m

Reverberation

"Persistence of sound caused by repeated reflections in enclosed space."

Problem: Excessive reverberation in auditoriums distorts clarity.

Solution: Use sound-absorbing materials:

  • Compressed fibre boards on walls/ceilings
  • Rough plaster surfaces
  • Draperies and curtains
  • Sound-absorbing seats
Difference between Echo and Reverberation:

Echo: Single reflection, distinct repetition

Reverberation: Multiple reflections, persistence/lingering of sound

SONAR System

What is SONAR?

SONAR = Sound Navigation And Ranging

Definition: Technique using ultrasonic waves to measure distance, direction, and speed of underwater objects.

Transmitter

Sends ultrasonic waves

Detector

Receives reflected waves

d = (v × t) / 2

Where: d = depth/distance, v = speed in water, t = time interval

Q: Ultrasound returns from seabed after 3.42s. Speed in seawater = 1531 m/s. Distance to seabed?

d = (v × t)/2 = (1531 × 3.42)/2

d = 5236.02/2 = 2618.01 m

Communication

Human Ear Structure

Parts of Ear

Part Function
Outer Ear (Pinna) Collects sound waves
Auditory Canal Directs sound to eardrum
Eardrum (Tympanic) Vibrates with sound waves
Middle Ear Bones Hammer, anvil, stirrup - amplify vibrations
Cochlea (Inner Ear) Converts vibrations to electrical signals
Auditory Nerve Sends signals to brain

Working: Sound waves → Eardrum vibrations → Bone amplification → Cochlea conversion → Nerve signals → Brain interpretation

Practice Questions

Problem Set

Q: Why hear humming bees but not pendulum vibrations?

Bee humming frequency (~200 Hz) is within audible range (20-20,000 Hz).

Pendulum frequency (< 20 Hz) is infrasound, below human hearing range.

Q: Flash & thunder produced simultaneously but thunder heard later. Why?

Light travels much faster (3×10⁸ m/s) than sound (~340 m/s).

Thunder is heard after lightning is seen due to slower speed of sound.

Q: Echo heard faster on hot day than cold day. Why?

Speed of sound increases with temperature.

Hot day → Higher temperature → Faster sound → Shorter echo time.

Q: Why dog suitable for detective work?

Dogs can hear ultrasound (up to 45,000 Hz).

Can detect sounds humans cannot hear.

Q: Frequency = 100 Hz. Vibrations per minute?

100 Hz = 100 vibrations/second

Per minute = 100 × 60 = 6000 vibrations

Multiple Choice Questions

MCQs with Answers

1. When sound travels through air, particles:

a) Move permanently

b) Vibrate along wave direction

c) Vibrate perpendicular to wave

d) Don't move

(b) Vibrate along wave direction - Sound waves are longitudinal

2. Speed of sound is maximum in:

a) Solids b) Liquids c) Gases d) Vacuum

(a) Solids - Fastest in solids, then liquids, slowest in gases

3. Loudness depends on:

a) Frequency b) Amplitude c) Wavelength d) Velocity

(b) Amplitude - Larger amplitude = louder sound

4. Time period = 0.01s. Frequency?

a) 10 Hz b) 50 Hz c) 100 Hz d) 200 Hz

ν = 1/T = 1/0.01 = 100 Hz

(c) 100 Hz

5. Human audible range:

a) 0-1000 Hz b) 20-20,000 Hz c) 50-15,000 Hz d) 20-50,000 Hz

(b) 20-20,000 Hz
