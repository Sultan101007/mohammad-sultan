Introduction

What is Motion?

"Motion is the change in position of an object with respect to its surroundings in a given time."

Rest

Object's position doesn't change with time

Example: Book on table, parked car

Motion

Object's position changes with time

Example: Moving car, flying bird

Force

Definition & Properties

"Force is a push or pull on a body that can change its state of motion or rest, or change its shape."

SI Unit: Newton (N)

CGS Unit: Dyne

1 N = 100,000 dyne

Effects of Force:
  • Can make stationary body move
  • Can stop moving body
  • Can change direction of moving object
  • Can change speed of moving body
  • Can change shape and size of object

Resultant Force

Net Force

"The resultant force is the total force acting on an object when multiple forces are combined. Also called net force."

Key Concept:

Resultant force = Vector sum of all forces acting on an object

If resultant force = 0 ‚Üí Balanced forces (no change in motion)

If resultant force ‚â† 0 ‚Üí Unbalanced forces (change in motion)

Galileo's Analysis on Motion

Experiments & Observations

Aristotle's Belief Galileo's Opposition
Natural state of bodies is rest Bodies naturally oppose changes
Objects need force to keep moving Objects continue moving without force (if no friction)
Galileo's Observations:
  • Ball rolling down inclined plane: Speed increases
  • Ball rolling up inclined plane: Speed decreases
  • On smooth horizontal plane: Ball continues moving with constant speed
Conclusion:

Bodies naturally oppose changes in their state of rest or motion. This property is called Inertia.

Inertia

Definition

"Inertia is the property of matter by which it tries to maintain its state of rest or uniform motion along a straight line."

Key Points:
  • Inertia is measured by mass
  • Inertia ‚àù Mass (directly proportional)
  • More mass = More inertia
  • Less mass = Less inertia

Inertia of Rest

An object stays at rest until external force affects it.

Example:

When a car accelerates suddenly, passengers feel pushed backward. Actually, their bodies tend to stay at rest due to inertia while the car moves forward.

Inertia of Motion

An object continues in motion until a force acts on it.

Example:

A hockey puck slides on ice until friction or another force stops it.

Inertia Examples

Q: Why does a person fall backward when bus starts suddenly?

Answer: Legs move with bus, but upper body tends to stay at rest due to inertia.

Q: Why does a person fall forward when bus brakes suddenly?

Answer: Bus stops but upper body tends to continue moving due to inertia.

Q: Why use seat belts?

Answer: Prevent forward movement during sudden stops (inertia of motion).

Newton's First Law of Motion

Statement

"A body remains in the state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line unless and until an external force acts on it."

Two Conditions:
  1. Object at rest: v = 0, a = 0 ‚Üí Stays at rest
  2. Object in motion: v ‚â† 0, a = 0 ‚Üí Continues with constant velocity

Newton's First Law = Law of Inertia

Momentum

Definition & Formula

"Momentum is the quantity of motion an object has."

\[p = m \times v\]

Where:
p = momentum (kg m/s)
m = mass (kg)
v = velocity (m/s)

Impulse:

Impulse = Force √ó Time

Impulse = Change in momentum

Momentum Examples

Why does a small bullet kill?

Answer: High velocity gives it large momentum (p = mv).

Why does moving vehicle cause severe injury?

Answer: Large mass √ó velocity = large momentum.

Newton's Second Law of Motion

Statement

"The rate of change of momentum of an object is directly proportional to the applied unbalanced force in the direction of the force."

\[F \propto \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}\]

\[F \propto \frac{m(v-u)}{t}\]

\[F \propto m \times a\]

\[F = k \times m \times a \quad (k = 1)\]

\[F = m \times a\]

Force vs Momentum

Force (F = ma) Momentum (p = mv)
Can exist when object stationary Zero for stationary objects
Direction depends on acceleration Direction depends on velocity
F ‚àù 1/t (inversely proportional) p ‚àù t (directly proportional)

Applications

Q: Why does fielder pull hands back while catching?

Answer: Increases time ‚Üí decreases acceleration ‚Üí reduces force (F = ma).

Q: Why fall on sand instead of concrete?

Answer: Sand increases time of fall ‚Üí reduces force of impact.

Q: Why karate player uses quick strike?

Answer: High acceleration ‚Üí large force (F = ma).

Newton's Third Law of Motion

Statement

"To every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction."

\[F_{1} = -F_{2}\]

Action and reaction forces are:
‚Ä¢ Equal in magnitude
‚Ä¢ Opposite in direction
‚Ä¢ Act on different bodies

Examples

Q: Why do both players get hurt in collision?

Answer: Each exerts equal opposite force on other (action-reaction).

Q: Why does gun recoil when fired?

Answer: Gun pushes bullet forward, bullet pushes gun backward.

Q: Why does rocket move upward?

Answer: Rocket pushes gases down, gases push rocket up.

Q: Why swimmer pushes water backward?

Answer: To get forward push from water (reaction).

Real-life Applications

Transportation

  • Seat belts: Prevent injury due to inertia during sudden stops
  • Airbags: Increase time of impact ‚Üí reduce force
  • Crumple zones: Increase collision time ‚Üí reduce force on passengers

Sports

  • Cricket: Fielder pulls hands back to catch
  • High jump: Fall on cushioned beds
  • Running: Push ground backward to move forward

Daily Life

  • Shaking wet clothes: Water droplets fall due to inertia
  • Hammer tightening: Inertia of hammer head
  • Coin in glass trick: Inertia of rest

Answers

Detailed Answers

Q1:

Rock has more inertia because inertia ‚àù mass. Rock has greater mass than football of same size.

Q2:

\[p = m \times v = 5 \times 0.20 = 1 \, \text{kg m/s}\]

Q3:

Total mass = 4000 + 20000 = 24000 kg
\[p = m \times v = 24000 \times 2 = 48000 \, \text{kg m/s}\]

Q4:

1 Newton = Force that gives 1 kg mass acceleration of 1 m/s¬≤
Using F = ma ‚Üí a = (v-u)/t = (5-2)/10 = 0.3 m/s¬≤
\[m = F/a = 2/0.3 = 6.67 \, \text{kg}\]

Q5:

Total mass = 8000 + (5√ó2000) = 8000 + 10000 = 18000 kg
Net force = Engine force - Friction = 40000 - 5000 = 35000 N

Q6:

Earth also attracts moon with equal force. Both move around their common center of mass. Earth's mass is much larger, so its acceleration is negligible.

Q7:

m = 10 g = 0.01 kg, u = 0, v = 400 m/s, t = 0.004 s
\[a = (v-u)/t = 400/0.004 = 100000 \, \text{m/s}^2\]
\[F = ma = 0.01 \times 100000 = 1000 \, \text{N}\]
