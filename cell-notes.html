Introduction to Cell

What is a Cell?

"Cells are the structural, functional, and biological units of all living beings."

  • The entire functioning of living organisms begins from the basic unit called cell, that's why it's called the fundamental unit of life.
  • A cell can replicate itself independently. Hence, they are known as the building blocks of life.
  • Cells provide structure and support, facilitate growth, allow transport, produce energy, and aid in reproduction.
Key Concept:

Just like bricks make a building, cells make an organism. Each cell carries out specific functions while working together in harmony.

Answer: Robert Brown discovered the cell nucleus in 1831.

Cell Theory

Main Principles

The Cell Theory (Schleiden & Schwann, 1838-39):
  1. All living organisms are composed of cells.
  2. The living organisms could be made up of one or more cells.
  3. The new cells arise from pre-existing cells through the process of cell division.

Modern Addition: Cells contain hereditary information (DNA) which is passed from parent to daughter cells during cell division.

Single cell organisms

Examples: Bacteria, Protozoa, Amoeba

Multiple cell organisms

Examples: Humans, Animals, Plants

Cell Shape

  • Variable shapes: Cells can be spherical, spindle-shaped, elongated, branched, discoidal, polygonal, etc.
  • Function determines shape: Nerve cells are long to conduct impulses, red blood cells are disc-shaped for easy flow.
  • Plant cells: Generally rectangular due to rigid cell wall.
  • Animal cells: Irregular shapes depending on function.

Plasma Membrane

Structure and Functions

  • Outermost covering of the cell that separates cell contents from external environment.
  • Selectively permeable: Allows entry and exit of some materials, prevents movement of others.
  • Made up of lipids and proteins (fluid mosaic model).
  • Allows diffusion of substances like carbon dioxide and oxygen.
  • Regulates movement of substances in and out of the cell.
  • Provides shape and protection to the cell.
Cell Wall (Plants only):
  • Non-living, rigid outer covering composed mainly of cellulose.
  • Provides structural strength and lies outside the plasma membrane.
  • Permits plants to resist bursting in hypotonic environments.

Osmosis

"Osmosis is the passage of water molecules across a semi-permeable membrane from a solution with a high concentration to a solution with a lower concentration."

  • Endosmosis: Water enters the cell (cell swells)
  • Exosmosis: Water moves out of cell (cell shrinks)
  • Plasmolysis: Shrinkage of cell contents away from cell wall in plants due to water loss

Diffusion vs Osmosis

Diffusion Osmosis
Movement of particles from high to low concentration Movement of solvent across semipermeable membrane
Occurs in liquids and gases Occurs in liquids only
No membrane required Requires semi-permeable membrane
Water not necessary Water is main solvent
Flow in all directions Flow in one direction only
Example: Ink in water Example: RBC swelling in water

Q: Lipid molecules in the cell are synthesised by:

(a) Smooth endoplasmic reticulum (b) Rough endoplasmic reticulum (c) Golgi apparatus (d) Plastids

Answer: (a) Smooth endoplasmic reticulum

Golgi Apparatus

  • Named after Camillo Golgi.
  • Comprises membrane-bound vesicles arranged in stacks (cisterns).
  • Functions: Packaging, storage, modification and transport of proteins.
  • Plays role in formation of lysosomes.
  • Known as the 'Traffic police' of the cell.

Lysosomes

  • Filled with digestive enzymes from RER.
  • Acts as the cell's waste disposal system.
  • Digests foreign material and worn-out organelles.
  • Termed "suicide bags" - burst during cellular disturbance causing self-digestion.
  • Breaks down all organic material including bacteria.

Mitochondria

  • Cell's powerhouses.
  • Two membranes: porous outer and folded inner (cristae).
  • Inner folds increase surface area for ATP generation.
  • Produces ATP (energy currency of cell).
  • Has its own DNA and ribosomes (semi-autonomous).

Plastids

  • Exclusive to plant cells.
  • Chromoplasts: Colored plastids (flowers, fruits).
  • Chloroplasts: Green chromoplasts containing chlorophyll for photosynthesis.
  • Leucoplasts: Colorless plastids for storage (starch, oils, proteins).

Ribosomes

  • Membrane-less cell organelle.
  • Involved in synthesis of proteins.
  • Present in both Prokaryotic and Eukaryotic cells.
  • Made of rRNA and proteins.
Q: Which of these options are not the functions of ribosomes?

I. It helps in manufacture of protein molecules.
II. It helps in manufacture of enzymes.
III. It helps in manufacture of hormones.
IV. It helps in manufacture of starch molecules.

(a) I and II (b) I and III (c) III and IV (d) IV and I

Answer: (c) III and IV

Vacuoles

  • Storage sacs in cells.
  • Small in animals, large in plants.
  • Plant central vacuole occupies 50-90% of cell volume.
  • Filled with cell sap (amino acids, sugars, proteins).
  • Provides turgidity and rigidity to plant cells.
  • In single-celled organisms: stores food, expels excess water and wastes.

Q: Why cell walls are only present in plants and not animals?

Answer: Cell wall provides rigidity and fixed structure to plants which are immobile. It contains cellulose making trunks and branches strong. Animals need flexibility for movement.

Cell Division

Introduction

  • Process through which new cells are formed in organisms.
  • New cells formed for growth, cell replacement, and reproduction.
  • Two main types: Mitosis and Meiosis.

Mitosis vs Meiosis

Equational division Reductional division

Same chromosome number as parent Half chromosome number as parent
Helps in growth and repair Responsible for gamete production
Occurs in somatic cells Occurs in germ cells
One division cycle Two division cycles

Practice Questions (PYQ)

Previous Year Questions

Q1: In brief state what happens when:

(a) Dry apricots are left for sometime in pure water and later transferred to sugar solution?

(b) A red blood cell is kept in concentrated saline solution?

(c) The plasma membrane of a cell breaks down?

(d) Rhoeo leaves are boiled in water first and then a drop of sugar syrup is put on it?

(e) Golgi apparatus is removed from the cell?

Q2: The undefined nuclear region of prokaryotes are also known as:

(a) nucleus (b) nucleolus (c) nucleic acid (d) nucleoid

Q3: Lipid molecules in the cell are synthesised by:

(a) Smooth endoplasmic reticulum (b) Rough endoplasmic reticulum (c) Golgi apparatus (d) Plastids

Q4: Which of these options are not the functions of ribosomes?

I. It helps in manufacture of protein molecules.
II. It helps in manufacture of enzymes.
III. It helps in manufacture of hormones.
IV. It helps in manufacture of starch molecules.

(a) I and II (b) I and III (c) III and IV (d) IV and I

PYQ Answers

Detailed Answers

Q1 Answers:

(a) Dry apricots in pure water: Swell due to osmosis, then shrink in sugar solution due to exosmosis.

(b) Red blood cell in concentrated saline solution: Shrinks due to exosmosis caused by water loss.

(c) Plasma membrane breakage: Disruption of cell integrity, scattering of cell organelles.

(d) Boiled Rhoeo leaves with sugar syrup: No osmosis due to cell death, demonstrating selective permeability.

(e) Removal of Golgi apparatus: Impaired protein processing, storage, and secretion.

Other Answers:

Q2: (d) nucleoid

Q3: (a) Smooth endoplasmic reticulum

Q4: (c) III and IV
